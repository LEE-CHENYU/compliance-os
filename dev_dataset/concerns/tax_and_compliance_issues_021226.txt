Threshold: $78,000 aggregate per CALENDAR YEAR from a single foreign person.

REPORTING PERIOD & DATE RULES:
  - Form 3520 uses the CALENDAR YEAR (Jan 1 – Dec 31) as the measurement
    period. Each year is independent.
  - The operative date is the DATE RECEIVED by the U.S. person — i.e., the
    date funds are credited/posted to the U.S. bank account, NOT the date
    the foreign sender initiates the wire.
  - For international wires that typically take 1-3 business days, a wire
    sent from China on 12/30 but posted to the U.S. account on 1/2 would
    count toward the FOLLOWING calendar year.
  - The dates in our bank statements (cleared/posted dates) are the received
    dates and are the correct basis for Form 3520 measurement.
  - Form 3520 for a given tax year is due with the income tax return
    (April 15 of the following year, extendable with Form 4868).
    Both 2024 and 2025 Form 3520 (if needed) are due April 15, 2026.

B. Form 8938 — FATCA (Foreign Account Tax Compliance Act) [HIGH]

   RULE: U.S. persons must report specified foreign financial assets on
   Form 8938 if the total value exceeds these thresholds:

     Single filer living in the US:
       >$39,000 on the LAST DAY of the tax year, OR
       >$58,500 at ANY TIME during the tax year

     Married filing jointly living in the US:
       >$78,000 on the last day, OR >$117,000 at any time

     Single filer living ABROAD:
       >$156,000 on the last day, OR >$234,000 at any time

   "Specified foreign financial assets" is BROADER than FBAR — includes:
     - Foreign bank accounts

     - Interest in a foreign entity

C. Delinquent Filing Procedures [INFO]

   If FBAR or Form 8938 was not filed for prior years, the IRS offers:

   1. Streamlined Filing Compliance (if non-willful):
      - File last 3 years of returns + last 6 years of FBARs
      - Pay 5% penalty on highest aggregate foreign account balance
      - Certify that failure was non-willful

   2. Delinquent FBAR Submission Procedures:
      - File late FBARs through BSA E-Filing with explanation
      - No penalty if IRS hasn't contacted you about it
      - Include statement explaining why filing is late

   3. Delinquent Form 8938:
      - File with amended return or next return
      - Attach reasonable cause statement

E. STEM OPT Self-Employment — Legal Risk Analysis [CRITICAL]
   Added: 02/13/2026 (research findings)

   1. THE CORE VIOLATION RISK
      STEM OPT (24-month extension) does NOT permit traditional self-employment.
      Unlike regular 12-month post-completion OPT (which allows self-employment),
      STEM OPT requires:
        - Bona fide employer-employee relationship (employer controls what/how/when)
        - Employer enrolled in E-Verify
        - Signed I-983 training plan with specific learning objectives
        - Student may NOT provide employer attestations on their own behalf

      An LLC where the student controls everything generally FAILS the employer-
      employee test. If business was conducted through the LLC during STEM OPT
      without a proper I-983 and E-Verify employer backing it — that is potentially
      unauthorized employment.

   2. MISREPRESENTATION FLAG RISK — INA §212(a)(6)(C)(i)
      OPT misrepresentation flags are government notations indicating possible
      false information, typically discovered during:
        - Visa stamping at a consulate
        - Border reentry / port of entry inspection
        - Green card processing (I-485 / consular processing)
        - H-1B extension adjudication

      Triggers include:
        - Employment with companies later deemed fraudulent
        - Providing false information about OPT employment
        - Self-employment or unauthorized work activity that doesn't match
          what was reported in SEVP/SEVIS records
        - Reporting discrepancy: SEVIS shows employer A, but Schedule C / LLC
          filings show independent business activity

      Key point: The underlying unauthorized employment violation is serious,
      but a §212(a)(6)(C)(i) MISREPRESENTATION finding (concealing or lying
      about it) is MUCH harder to waive and can trigger a PERMANENT
      inadmissibility bar.

   3. KNOWN SUCCESSFUL MITIGATIONS
      No published pattern of "did unauthorized work on STEM OPT and got it
      forgiven." Strategies used by immigration attorneys:

      a. "Not employment" argument — If LLC activity was passive/de minimis
         (holding assets, no active services rendered), argue it doesn't meet
         the definition of unauthorized employment under 8 CFR 214.2(f).

      b. Regular OPT vs STEM OPT timing — Regular 12-month post-completion
         OPT DOES allow self-employment. If the LLC activity fell during the
         initial 12-month OPT period (not the STEM extension), different rules
         apply. CRITICAL: Map exact timeline of LLC activity against OPT dates.

      c. Nunc pro tunc — Discretionary remedy to retroactively reinstate or
         fix status. Very case-specific; requires showing equitable factors.

      d. Proactive remediation narrative — Document the situation now with a
         lawyer, build a factual record BEFORE USCIS ever asks, so there is a
         prepared response during H-1B extension or green card filing.

      e. Minimize exposure surface — Quantify exactly what happened: duration,
         revenue ($323 in 2023), activity type, whether it was truly "work" vs
         passive investment returns.

      Precedent case (Sabrina Li Law): Startup founder got NOID on STEM OPT;
      firm proved bona fide employer-employee relationship by distinguishing
      employee role from ownership role. Approved in 2 days. However, this was
      a prospective case (defending a pending application), not retroactive
      remediation of past unauthorized work.

   4. DISCLOSURE STRATEGY — KEY QUESTION FOR COUNSEL
      Whether to proactively disclose the OPT-period LLC activity or not is
      the central strategic decision. Arguments on both sides:

      AGAINST proactive disclosure:
        - No legal obligation to volunteer past violations unprompted
        - USCIS may never cross-reference LLC records, tax returns, and SEVIS
        - Disclosing creates a paper trail where none currently exists
        - Cannot un-disclose once disclosed

      FOR disclosure (or at least preparation):
        - If USCIS discovers it later (H-1B extension, green card, visa stamp),
          the cover-up is worse than the underlying violation
        - §212(a)(6)(C)(i) misrepresentation (concealing when asked) carries a
          PERMANENT inadmissibility bar — much worse than unauthorized employment
        - Schedule C income, LLC formation records, state filings are discoverable
        - Proactive framing controls the narrative and timing

      RECOMMENDED MIDDLE GROUND (subject to attorney advice):
        - Do NOT volunteer in filings unprompted
        - DO prepare a full factual record privately with immigration counsel NOW
        - Have a documented response strategy ready if ever asked
        - NEVER lie if directly questioned — that is the §212(a)(6)(C)(i) trigger
        - Structure future filings (H-1B extensions, green card petitions) to
          minimize exposure to questions that could surface the issue

   5. DISCOVERABILITY FACTORS — MAP WITH ATTORNEY
      How likely is USCIS to find the LLC activity? Depends on:
        - Schedule C on 2023 tax return ($323 revenue, $1,211 expenses)

XI. RELEVANT COURT CASES & PRECEDENTS
================================================================================

The following cases are directly relevant to the issues in this register.
They establish (or are actively shaping) the rules around IRS penalty
authority, reasonable cause defenses, and foreign reporting obligations.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

A. Huang v. United States [ONGOING — most directly relevant]
   Court: U.S. District Court, N.D. California
   Case No. 24-cv-06298-RS
   Status: ACTIVE — proceeding to merits (motion to dismiss denied May 2025)

   FACTS: Ms. Jiaxing Huang, a Chinese national who relocated to the U.S.,
   received substantial gifts from her non-resident foreign parents in 2015
   and 2016 to purchase a home. She used TurboTax to file her returns.
   TurboTax advised that recipients of foreign gifts do not need to report
   them — only the gift-giver does. In 2018, she learned of Form 3520 and
   promptly filed late. The IRS assessed penalties of $48,747.08 (2015)
   and $22,419.15 (2016) — totaling ~$71K. She requested abatement for
   reasonable cause; the IRS denied it.

   RULING SO FAR: The court DENIED the government's motion to dismiss on
   the reasonable cause claim. The court held that reliance on tax software
   (TurboTax) may constitute reasonable cause for failing to file Form 3520.
   The case will proceed to a full merits hearing.

   WHY THIS MATTERS FOR US:
     - Nearly identical fact pattern: Chinese family gifts to U.S. person
     - Establishes that reliance on tax software/preparer can be reasonable
       cause for Form 3520 penalties
     - If Huang prevails, it strengthens the defense for anyone who was
       unaware of Form 3520 requirements and filed late
     - Our situation: if Form 3520 is filed late for 2024 or 2025, a
       reasonable cause statement citing unawareness of the requirement
       (especially for someone who used standard tax software or a
       non-specialist preparer) has precedent support
     - The IRS also changed policy in 2025: no longer auto-assessing
       Form 3520 penalties — manual review required if reasonable cause
       statement is attached

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

B. Farhy v. Commissioner [CIRCUIT SPLIT — potential Supreme Court case]
   Tax Court: 160 T.C. No. 6 (April 3, 2023)
   D.C. Circuit: Reversed (May 3, 2024)
   Tax Court: Reaffirmed its position in Mukhi (Nov 18, 2024)
   Supreme Court: Certiorari petition expected — circuit split exists

   ISSUE: Does the IRS have statutory authority to ASSESS penalties under
   IRC §6038(b) for failure to file international information returns
   (Form 5471), or must it sue the taxpayer in court to collect?

   WHAT HAPPENED:
     - Tax Court (2023): Ruled the IRS CANNOT automatically assess Form
       5471 penalties. IRC §6038(b) does not grant assessment authority.
       The IRS must file a collection suit in federal court instead.
     - D.C. Circuit (2024): REVERSED — held that Congress intended §6038(b)
       penalties to be assessable. IRS CAN assess them.
     - Tax Court (Nov 2024, in Mukhi): REAFFIRMED its original position
       that IRS lacks assessment authority, creating a circuit split.
     - Rehearing denied; Supreme Court petition likely.

   WHY THIS MATTERS FOR US:
     - The Farhy reasoning MAY extend to penalties under Forms 5472, 8938,
       8865, and 926 — all of which use similar statutory language
     - If the Supreme Court sides with the Tax Court, the IRS would need
       to SUE taxpayers in federal court to collect these penalties,
       rather than simply assessing them administratively
     - This would make it significantly harder for the IRS to enforce
       penalties for late/missing Form 5472 or Form 5471
     - Current status: in Tax Court jurisdiction, penalties may be
       unassessable; in D.C. Circuit, they are assessable
     - PRACTICAL IMPACT: Even if penalties apply, the IRS enforcement
       mechanism is actively being litigated. This creates leverage
       for reasonable cause arguments and penalty abatement requests.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

C. Mukhi v. Commissioner [Form 3520 penalties upheld, Form 5471 not]
   Tax Court: 163 T.C. No. 8 (November 18, 2024)
   Status: Decided — but contributes to Farhy circuit split

   FACTS: Taxpayer established foreign corporations and foreign trusts,
   transferred >$7 million to them, and pleaded guilty to filing false
   tax returns and FBARs. IRS sought ~$9 million in Form 3520/3520-A
   penalties and $93,600 in Form 5471 penalties.

   RULINGS:
     - Form 3520/3520-A penalties ($9M): UPHELD. The court rejected
       constitutional challenges including:
         * Due Process (5th Amendment): rejected
         * Excessive Fines (8th Amendment): rejected — court held Form
           3520 penalties are "remedial" (to encourage compliance), not
           "punitive," so the Excessive Fines Clause does not apply
         * Equal Protection: rejected
     - Form 5471 penalties ($94K): IRS CANNOT ASSESS — reaffirming Farhy

   WHY THIS MATTERS FOR US:
     - BAD NEWS: Form 3520 penalties are enforceable and constitutional,
       even at very high amounts ($9M in this case). The Excessive Fines
       argument does not work for Form 3520.
     - GOOD NEWS: This was an extreme case (criminal conviction, $7M+
       in hidden assets). Our situation (family gifts, non-willful
       unawareness) is far more sympathetic for reasonable cause.
     - The Form 5472 penalty question remains open — Farhy/Mukhi logic
       about assessment authority could potentially extend to §6038A
       penalties (Form 5472), though this hasn't been directly tested.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

D. Bittner v. United States [DECIDED — sets FBAR penalty precedent]
   Supreme Court: 598 U.S. 85 (February 28, 2023)
   Status: DECIDED — binding precedent

   FACTS: Alexandru Bittner, a dual Romanian-U.S. citizen, returned to
   the U.S. in 2011 and learned of his FBAR obligations. He filed late
   reports for 2007-2011 covering 272 foreign accounts. The IRS assessed
   penalties at $7,800 PER ACCOUNT × 272 = $2.12 million.

   RULING: 5-4 decision in FAVOR of taxpayer. Non-willful FBAR penalties
   apply PER REPORT (per year), NOT per account.
     - Result: penalty reduced from ~$2.12M to ~$39K (5 years × $8K)

   WHY THIS MATTERS FOR US:
     - If FBAR is filed late for 2024 (Futu account), the maximum
       non-willful penalty is $12,898 per REPORT (one per year), NOT
       per account
     - Even with multiple foreign accounts, the penalty is capped at
       the per-report amount for non-willful violations
     - HOWEVER: if the IRS determines the violation is "willful," the
       per-report cap does NOT apply — penalties can be up to 50% of
       account balance or $128,975 per account
     - For our case: one account (Futu), likely non-willful → max
       exposure ~$12,898 per year of non-filing

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

E. IRS Policy Change (2025) — Form 3520 Penalty Assessment [INFO]

   Starting 2025, the IRS will NO LONGER automatically assess penalties
   for late-filed Form 3520. Instead:
     - If a reasonable cause statement is included with a late filing,
       a human reviewer must evaluate it before any penalty is issued
     - This is a significant shift from the prior practice of automatic
       computer-generated penalty notices
     - Effectively means: file late with a good reasonable cause letter
       → much better chance of penalty abatement than before

   Combined with Huang (TurboTax defense surviving dismissal), this
   creates a favorable environment for filing delinquent Form 3520s
   with reasonable cause explanations.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

SUMMARY — CASE LAW IMPACT ON OUR SITUATION:

  Form 3520 (foreign gifts):
    → File with reasonable cause statement. Huang case supports
      "didn't know about requirement" defense. IRS no longer auto-
      assesses. Penalty abatement is realistic if filed proactively.

  Form 5472 (LLC reporting):
    → Likely not needed under Path A (U.S. resident, gift treatment).

      authority is being actively challenged. Penalties may be
      unassessable depending on jurisdiction.

  FBAR:
    → Bittner caps non-willful penalty at ~$12,898/year (per report).

      FILE 2024 FBAR IMMEDIATELY — do not wait for tax season.

  Form 8938:
    → No major favorable case law. File if thresholds are met.
      Penalty is $7,800 per failure. Reasonable cause defense available.
