  GUSTO RECORD CORRECTION: DO NOT AMEND (DECIDED)
  ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
    Question: Should Mendy correct the pay period dates in Gusto so the
    paystub reflects "June 2025" instead of "Oct 16 – Nov 17, 2025"?

    ANSWER: NO. Do NOT amend the Gusto records.

    REASONS:
      1. TIMESTAMP CREATES EVIDENCE OF MANIPULATION:
         Gusto logs all amendments with timestamps. A pay period change
         made in March 2026 — four months after the payroll was processed
         — creates a dated record that looks like retroactive manipulation
         rather than an honest correction. An adjudicator seeing "amended
         03/2026" on a Nov 2025 payroll would be more suspicious, not less.

      2. ORIGINAL PAYSTUB ALREADY EXISTS:
         The paystub PDF was generated at the time of processing and has
         been distributed. Chenyu has a copy. Bank deposits reference the
         Nov 18 pay date. The W-2 was generated from these records. The
         original cannot be un-created — amending Gusto only produces a
         SECOND version that contradicts the first, which is worse than
         having one version with a correction letter.

      3. GUSTO MAY NOT ALLOW IT:
         Processed off-cycle payrolls may not permit retroactive pay
         period date edits. Attempting and failing creates an audit log
         entry showing the attempt, which is worse than doing nothing.

      4. DRAWS ATTENTION:
         Making system-level changes signals awareness of a problem and
         active record modification. Leaving it alone and having a
         correction letter ready is far less conspicuous.

      5. EMPLOYER CORRECTION LETTER IS THE STANDARD REMEDY:
         Employers routinely issue correction letters for payroll errors.
         USCIS accepts employer letters as evidence. The letter + the
         corroborating delayed payroll pattern across all 7 paystubs is
         a stronger position than an obviously-backdated Gusto amendment.

    DECISION: Leave Gusto as-is. The pay period date is a documentation
    artifact that is best explained (when asked) rather than erased.

5. IS THIS A CRITICAL ISSUE?
================================================================================

  SHORT ANSWER: NO — this is a LOW-TO-MEDIUM risk issue that is very
  unlikely to affect the H-1B petition outcome.

  WHY IT IS NOT CRITICAL:

  1. WRONG EMPLOYER FOR H-1B:
     The H-1B petition is through BSGC LLC or Pacific River Capital, NOT
     Wolff & Li. USCIS adjudicates the petition based on the PETITIONING
     employer's qualifications and the beneficiary's eligibility. Wolff &
     Li is a prior/concurrent employer that is not part of the petition.

  2. USCIS DOES NOT ROUTINELY AUDIT PAYSTUBS:
     H-1B initial petitions require the employer to demonstrate the job
     offer, wage level, and beneficiary qualifications. They do NOT
     routinely require paystubs from OTHER employers. Paystubs would only
     come into play if USCIS issues an RFE specifically requesting
     employment history documentation.

  3. THE UNAUTHORIZED GAP ITSELF IS NOT DISQUALIFYING:
     A gap in employment authorization does not automatically bar H-1B
     eligibility. Under INA § 212(a)(6)(C)(i), unauthorized employment
     is grounds for inadmissibility only if the applicant made a material
     misrepresentation. A paystub with incorrect Gusto dates — where no
     actual work occurred during the gap — is not unauthorized employment.
     It is a payroll processing error.

     Even if USCIS viewed it as a potential violation, the standard for
     finding unauthorized employment requires ACTUAL unauthorized work,
     not a payroll artifact. The employer's confirmation that no work
     occurred during the gap directly rebuts any inference.

  4. STRONG CORROBORATING EVIDENCE:
     The delayed payroll pattern is not a one-off excuse — it is
     documented across ALL 7 paystubs with delays ranging from 2 days
     to 171 days. This makes the correction letter highly credible.

  5. PETITION TIMING:
     By the time the H-1B petition is filed (April-June 2026), the
     Wolff & Li CPT employment will have ended naturally (Apr 17, 2026).
     There will be no ongoing employment to scrutinize.

  SCENARIOS WHERE IT COULD BECOME AN ISSUE:

  A. RFE FOR EMPLOYMENT HISTORY:
     Likelihood: LOW (~10-15%)
     If USCIS requests a full employment history, Wolff & Li would need
     to be disclosed. The paystub dates could trigger follow-up questions.
     Response: Submit prepared correction letter package (Strategy B).

  B. BACKGROUND CHECK / SAVE DATABASE:
     Likelihood: VERY LOW (<5%)
     USCIS could cross-reference SEVIS records showing STEM OPT end date
     with E-Verify records or tax filings. However, E-Verify only shows
     initial verification (Mar 2025, already closed as "Employment
     Authorized"). W-2 shows total annual wages, not work periods.
     The paystub-level detail would not surface in a background check.

  C. CONSULAR PROCESSING (if applicable):
     Likelihood: NOT APPLICABLE
     Chenyu is filing for change of status (already in the U.S.), not
     consular processing. Consular officers tend to scrutinize employment
     history more closely, but this scenario does not apply.

  D. FUTURE GREEN CARD / ADJUSTMENT OF STATUS:
     Likelihood: MEDIUM (years from now)
     When filing I-485 (green card), USCIS reviews the applicant's full
     immigration history. At that point, the unauthorized gap could be
     examined more closely. However:
       - Years will have passed
       - The correction letter will already be on file
       - The H-1B approval itself is evidence that USCIS did not find
         unauthorized employment
       - The gap is short (47 days) with no actual unauthorized work

  BOTTOM LINE:
  ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
    This is a DOCUMENTATION issue, not an EMPLOYMENT issue.
    No unauthorized work actually occurred. The Gusto pay period dates
    are a payroll processing artifact. The risk is that the artifact
    LOOKS like unauthorized employment to a USCIS adjudicator who only
    sees the paystub face value.

    The mitigation is simple:
      1. Have the correction letter ready (DONE — draft prepared)
      2. Brief the petition attorney (TODO — at intake)
      3. Do not volunteer the issue (Strategy B)
      4. Minimize Wolff & Li in the filing (Strategy C)

    Probability of this affecting H-1B outcome: <5%
    Probability of this requiring any response at all: <15%
