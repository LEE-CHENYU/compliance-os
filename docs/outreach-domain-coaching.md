## The Foundation: One Mental Model Underlies Everything

**NRA vs. Resident Alien** — this distinction drives almost every cross-domain conflict Guardian catches.

| | Non-Resident Alien (NRA) | Resident Alien (RA) |
|---|---|---|
| Taxed on | US-source income only | Worldwide income |
| Tax return | 1040-NR | 1040 |
| FBAR required | No | Yes |
| Typical profile | F-1/J-1 students (first 5 years) | H-1B holders who've hit SPT |
| Treaty positions | More available | Some restrictions |

**Substantial Presence Test (SPT):**  
183+ days, calculated as: all days this year + ⅓ last year's days + ⅙ the year before.  
F-1/J-1 students are "exempt individuals" — their days don't count — but only for the first **5 calendar years** (F-1) or **2 calendar years** (J-1). In year 6/3, the clock starts. Most students don't know this.

**Why this matters for Guardian:** The moment someone crosses from NRA to RA, 4–5 compliance obligations change simultaneously — FBAR kicks in, worldwide income becomes taxable, RSU tax treatment changes, treaty positions may break. No single advisor tracks this transition across domains.

## Topic 1: Form 8843

**What it is:** An annual statement filed by F-1/J-1/M-1/Q visa holders telling the IRS "my days in the US don't count toward SPT." It is not a tax return — it's a residency election form.

**Due date:** June 15 (not April 15). Filed by mail only. No e-file option.

**Who must file:** Every F-1/J-1 student *and* their F-2/J-2 spouse and children — even with zero income, even if they're just dependents.

**What most people miss:**
- Must be filed every year in exempt status, not just once
- Missing it means those days retroactively count toward SPT
- Enough missed years = inadvertent resident alien status — back taxes, penalties, FBAR exposure going back years
- Separate from tax return. Separate from income. A student with $0 income still needs to file it.
- Your TA stipend, fellowship, or scholarship income is a separate question — 8843 is purely about residency

**Guardian angle:** The Form 8843 generator is the top-of-funnel wedge. Once you have their data, you can check whether they've been filing consistently and flag if they're approaching the 5-year exempt window.

## Topic 2: FBAR (FinCEN 114)

**What it is:** Annual report of foreign financial accounts. Any US "person" — including resident aliens, not just citizens — who holds >$10,000 in foreign accounts at *any point* during the year must file.

**Due:** April 15, auto-extended to October 15. Filed online with FinCEN (separate from IRS tax return).

**Penalties:**
- Non-willful violation: $10,000/year per account
- Willful violation: greater of $100,000 or 50% of account balance per year
- Criminal exposure for willful cases

**What most people miss:**
- The trigger is residency status, not citizenship. The moment someone becomes a resident alien (H-1B + SPT), FBAR applies — immediately — to any foreign accounts held all year
- The $10,000 threshold is the *aggregate* of all foreign accounts, not per account
- "Foreign account" includes accounts you're a signatory on — the joint account with parents in China counts
- Many H-1B holders on year 3 or 4 who became RA in year 2 have owed FBAR for years without knowing it
- Their employer's immigration attorney has zero visibility into their Chinese bank accounts

**Cross-domain conflict:** Immigration attorney files H-1B. Company HR processes W-2. Nobody tells the employee that SPT has been met and FBAR is now required on their parents' joint account in China. This is Guardian's sharpest edge for the H-1B tech worker segment.

## Topic 3: H-1B Compliance

**What it is:** Employer-sponsored work visa. Three-year initial period, renewable to six. Tied to specific employer, specific job title, specific worksite.

**Status vs. visa distinction:**  
The visa stamp in your passport can be expired while you remain in valid H-1B *status* in the US. Status is what matters for working legally. You only need a valid stamp when re-entering the US after international travel. Many H-1B holders don't understand this and think they're out of status when their stamp expires.

**Common compliance gaps workers carry unaware:**
- Remote work from home can require an amended LCA (Labor Condition Application) if home address is in a different metro than the approved worksite
- Changing job functions — even at the same employer — may require an amended petition if duties materially change
- Layoffs: 60-day grace period to find new employer, file new petition, or depart
- The employer's immigration attorney represents the *company*, not the employee — their job is to protect the company's liability, not advise the worker on cross-domain risks

**AC21 portability:**  
After an I-485 (adjustment of status) has been pending for 180+ days, the employee can change employers to a "same or similar" role without restarting the green card process. Widely misunderstood — many people stay in bad situations thinking they'll lose their place in the green card queue when they wouldn't.

**Conversation angle:** "Your employer's attorney's client is the company, not you. They filed your H-1B correctly. They have no visibility into your FBAR exposure, your RSU tax treatment as it shifts with residency, or whether your tax treaty election is still valid."

## Topic 4: OPT / STEM OPT

**OPT:** 12 months of work authorization for F-1 graduates. Not tied to a specific employer, but must work in a role related to your degree field.

**STEM OPT:** 24-month extension for STEM degree holders. Total: 36 months of work authorization before needing an H-1B.

**STEM OPT compliance requirements (commonly missed):**
- Employer must be E-Verify registered
- Student must submit Form I-983 (training plan) within 10 days of starting with new employer
- 6-month and annual self-evaluation reports required
- Student and employer supervisor must both sign evaluations

**Unemployment limits:**
- Regular OPT: 90 days total unemployment
- STEM OPT extension: 60 additional days (150 days combined total for OPT + STEM OPT)
- Exceeding these limits = out of status

**What most people miss:**  
Changing employers during STEM OPT requires a new I-983 *before* starting work. Many people switch jobs and start working without updating their SEVIS record. Technically out of status — affects H-1B eligibility and green card process downstream.

## Topic 5: 83(b) Election

**What it is:** A one-time election to pay income tax on restricted stock at the *grant date* (when value is often near zero) rather than at each vesting date (when value may be high). Must be filed with the IRS within **30 days of grant**. No exceptions. No extensions. No cure.

**Why it matters:** Without 83(b), you pay ordinary income tax each time a tranche vests at current FMV. With 83(b), you paid tax at grant on near-zero value, and subsequent appreciation is capital gains — taxed at lower rates, and potentially QSBS-excluded.

**What most people miss:**
- Must be mailed (keep USPS certified mail proof — IRS doesn't confirm receipt)
- Does not apply to RSUs — only restricted stock (common for founders, not employees with RSUs)
- Immigrant founders often don't think about whether the election was properly completed for their NRA vs. RA status at the time of grant
- For NRAs: Capital gains from US company stock are generally *not* taxed by the US. So the 83(b) calculus shifts — you're paying ordinary income tax at grant now to convert future gain into capital gain that's non-taxable as an NRA (or QSBS-excluded if you become RA before exit)
- If you become RA before exit, QSBS kicks in — you want the 83(b) done correctly from the start

**The 30-day window is the hardest deadline in startup law.** No grace period. No cure. Missing it means taxed at vest. Guardian should surface this check first for any founder.

## Topic 6: QSBS (Qualified Small Business Stock, §1202)

**What it is:** Up to $10M of capital gain (or 10× investment basis, whichever is greater) excluded from federal income tax on sale of qualifying stock.

**Requirements:**
- US C-corporation (not LLC, not S-corp)
- Company assets under $50M at time of stock issuance
- Stock acquired at original issuance (not secondary market)
- Held for at least 5 years

**What most people miss:**
- NRAs don't pay US capital gains tax on US stock anyway — so QSBS isn't what makes the analysis interesting for them. It becomes critical the moment they become a RA before exit.
- **California and New York do not conform to federal QSBS** — full state tax on the gain regardless
- The 5-year clock starts from grant, not vest — so a correctly done 83(b) and 5-year hold from grant qualifies, even if you're still vesting
- For immigrant founders transitioning from NRA to RA mid-hold: complex source-of-income allocation issues arise that most accountants don't model correctly

## Topic 7: Tax Treaties

**What they are:** Bilateral agreements between the US and 60+ countries reducing or eliminating certain taxes for residents of the other country.

**Key treaties for Guardian's audience:**

| Country | Key provision | Benefit |
|---|---|---|
| China | Article 20 | Students/trainees: exclude stipends/scholarships for 5 years |
| China | Article 19 | Professors/researchers: exclude teaching/research income for 3 years |
| Japan | Article 20 | Students: similar exemption |
| Korea | Article 21 | Students: similar exemption |
| Germany, UK | Various | Reduced dividend withholding, pension treatment |
| **India** | **None** | **No US-India income tax treaty — Indian H-1B holders get no treaty benefits** |

**What most people miss:**
- Treaty benefits are not automatic — you must *claim* them by filing Form 8833 and indicating your treaty position on your 1040-NR
- Many international students leave thousands of dollars on the table every year simply because nobody told them to file Form 8833
- There's a tension for H-1B holders: some treaty provisions require the person to not intend permanent residence. H-1B is "dual intent" — you can simultaneously hold H-1B and pursue a green card. But claiming certain treaty provisions can look inconsistent with immigrant intent. The IRS and USCIS share data.

**Conversation angle:** "If you're a Chinese student or researcher on F-1 or J-1, you're almost certainly owed a refund from previous years. Article 20 of the US-China treaty exempts your stipend from tax for 5 years. Most students never claim it."

## The Cross-Domain Conflict Matrix

This is Guardian's core pitch. Each trigger event creates compliance obligations across multiple domains that no single advisor sees together.

| Trigger event | Immigration sees | Tax sees | The gap nobody catches |
|---|---|---|---|
| F-1 year 6 begins | Status unchanged (still F-1) | SPT clock starts | FBAR now applies; worldwide income taxable; treaty positions may break |
| H-1B approved, 3rd year in US | Status maintained | Resident alien via SPT | FBAR required on all foreign accounts; worldwide income now taxable |
| Founder issues equity (day 1) | Visa type noted | 83(b) deadline running | Whether NRA/RA status affects election validity; QSBS eligibility window |
| STEM OPT → new employer | I-983 update required | W-2 changes employer | Training plan gap; OPT unemployment clock; SEVIS out of status risk |
| Company exit / liquidity event | Not immigration issue | Capital gains event | Was QSBS properly structured? Was 83(b) done correctly as NRA? Is FBAR current? |
| H-1B holder gets foreign inheritance | Not immigration issue | Possibly reportable | FBAR threshold crossed; Form 3520 (foreign gifts >$100K) may be required |

## Segment-Specific Talking Points

### YC / Immigrant Founders
> "The cross-domain risk for immigrant founders is that your immigration attorney, your tax accountant, and your equity attorney each only see one piece. Guardian reads all three together. The 83(b) window, your visa status at grant, your FBAR obligations after becoming a resident alien — those conflicts only show up when you check all three simultaneously."

**Questions to ask:**
- "When you got your founder shares, did anyone check that the 83(b) election was done correctly for your visa status at the time?"
- "When did you first hit Substantial Presence? Did anyone walk you through what changed on the tax side?"

### H-1B Tech Workers (Big Tech)
> "Your employer's immigration attorney's client is your company, not you. They filed your H-1B correctly — but they have no visibility into your Chinese bank accounts or whether you've been FBAR-compliant since you hit Substantial Presence. Guardian is the check that exists for you specifically."

**Questions to ask:**
- "Do you have bank accounts in China over $10K combined? Have you been filing FBAR since your third year in the US?"
- "Has anyone walked you through what changed on the tax side when you became a resident alien?"

### International Students
> "Form 8843 is due June 15 and most students have never heard of it. Missing it starts a clock that can make you a resident alien retroactively — which matters for your taxes now and your H-1B and green card eligibility later."

**Questions to ask:**
- "Have you been filing Form 8843 every year since you arrived? It's separate from your tax return."
- "Are you aware of the US-China Article 20 exemption? If you're Chinese and on a stipend, you may be owed a refund."

### Compliance-Adjacent Founders (Alma, Hibee, etc.)
> "We're building in adjacent layers. Guardian handles the cross-domain document check — immigration × tax × corporate together. You're solving [their problem]. Might be worth comparing notes on where the layers overlap."

## Three Conversation-Opening Questions

Memorize these. They work across almost every segment and open a real conversation rather than a pitch:

1. **"When did you first hit Substantial Presence? Did anyone walk you through what changed?"**  
   → Opens the NRA→RA transition, FBAR, treaty discussion for H-1B workers and students

2. **"When you got your founder shares, who checked the 83(b) election was done right for your visa status?"**  
   → Opens the equity × immigration conflict for founders

3. **"Do you have bank accounts in China over $10K combined? Have you been filing FBAR?"**  
   → Opens the most tangible, scary, immediate risk for Chinese immigrants on H-1B

These are peer-level questions. They demonstrate that you understand their situation before you've said a word about Guardian.
